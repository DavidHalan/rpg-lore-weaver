# Techniques and Examples

Detailed character creation techniques referenced by `rpg-lore-weaver`.

## The Wurg Brotën Example (Origin Technique)

A half-orc whose parents were murdered, raised by a Queen in secret. He learned art, music, and warfare, becoming a gentle knight in heavy armor who doesn't know how to talk to common people. His parents' death left a scar: "the dead are the lucky ones."

**Why it works:**

- Origin (orphaned half-orc) creates immediate sympathy
- Background (raised by royalty) creates contradiction and humor
- Key Decision (embracing gentle nature despite orcish blood) shows depth
- Weakness (socially awkward with commoners) makes him relatable
- Legacy phrase ("the dead are the lucky ones") gives the DM endless hooks

**Takeaway**: History creates consequences. Every detail in the past echoes in the present.

## Turning Low Stats Into Lived Weaknesses

Don't just accept a number — **narrate the reason behind it**.

| Low Stat     | Mechanical Effect     | Narrative Version                                                                           |
| ------------ | --------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Dexterity    | -1 to AC, saves       | A stiff leg from a childhood fall. Companions instinctively help.                           |
| Intelligence | Poor knowledge checks | Slower speech, simpler words. "What does that word mean?" becomes a catchphrase.            |
| Charisma     | Failed persuasion     | Painfully shy? Arrogant? Speaks too bluntly and doesn't understand why people are offended? |
| Wisdom       | Poor perception       | Naive, trusting. Takes people at their word. Gets swindled repeatedly.                      |
| Strength     | Can't carry much      | Small frame, old injury, or deliberate refusal to use force.                                |
| Constitution | Low HP                | Chronic illness, malnourishment in childhood, or a curse that slowly drains vitality.       |

**The Key Insight**: The moment the character stops fighting the weakness and starts embracing it is the moment they grow stronger narratively.

## The Crossroads Technique (Key Decisions)

For every major backstory decision, write **the choice AND the consequence**:

| Crossroad                    | Choice Made              | Consequence                               |
| ---------------------------- | ------------------------ | ----------------------------------------- |
| King demanded obedience      | Defied the crown         | Exiled, now hunts bounty                  |
| Lover asked them to stay     | Fled in the night        | Ended up in a monastery                   |
| Enemy was defeated in combat | Chose revenge over mercy | Lost their best friend who couldn't watch |
| Found forbidden knowledge    | Read the tome            | Gained power but attracts fiends          |

**Rule of Thumb**: 2-3 crossroads is enough to make a character feel impossibly real.

## The Sensory Memory Technique

When probing the user about traumatic or important events, ask for **sensory details**, not plot summaries:

Instead of asking:

> "What happened when your parents died?"

Ask:

> "What do you remember most about that moment — a sound, a smell, a silence? The crack of wood? The smell of smoke? The weight of someone's hand on your shoulder?"

Sensory details anchor the backstory in emotion rather than exposition.

## The Contradiction Engine

The most memorable characters have internal contradictions. Use these as prompts:

- A **pacifist warrior** who trains every day for fights they never want to have
- A **cynical healer** who saves lives but doesn't believe in people
- A **cowardly rogue** who appears brave only because they're too scared to run
- A **devout cleric** who secretly doubts their god's existence
- A **gentle barbarian** who writes poetry between battles
- A **cheerful necromancer** who genuinely loves the undead they raise

**Why contradictions work**: They create internal tension, which generates roleplay opportunities without external plot.

## The Real-World Inspiration Technique

Base characters on **real people, historical figures, or fictional characters you admire** — then twist them into something new.

**The process:**

1. Pick a real person or character that fascinates you
2. Identify the ONE trait that makes them compelling
3. Transplant that trait into a completely different context
4. Let the new context transform the character into something original

| Inspiration                      | Core Trait                               | RPG Translation                                                                                        |
| -------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| A grandmother who survived a war | Quiet resilience, refuses to complain    | Dwarven cleric who heals without ever acknowledging her own wounds                                     |
| A street musician you saw once   | Joy despite poverty, performs for no one | Bard who plays for the ghosts of a battlefield, not for the living                                     |
| Sherlock Holmes                  | Brilliant but emotionally distant        | Wizard who solves mysteries but can't understand why people cry                                        |
| A strict but caring teacher      | Tough love, high standards               | Paladin mentor who pushes recruits to breaking point — because she knows what waits for the unprepared |

**Key insight**: Originality doesn't come from inventing something no one has ever seen. It comes from **how the details combine**. A fire mage who's afraid of the dark isn't original in pieces — but together, it's a person.

> **Prompt to use**: "Is there a real person — someone you know, a historical figure, a character from a book or film — whose energy you'd want this character to carry?"

## The Cliché Launchpad Technique

**Clichés are not failures — they are foundations.** The orphan, the chosen one, the brooding loner — these exist because they _work_. The trick is not to avoid them, but to **launch from them into unexpected territory.**

**The method:**

1. Start with a cliché (openly, without shame)
2. Ask: "What if this cliché went somewhere unexpected?"
3. Add one detail that subverts or deepens the archetype

| Cliché                      | Subversion                                                     | Result                                            |
| --------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------- |
| Orphan seeking revenge      | The villain is their sibling — and had good reasons            | Character must reconcile grief with understanding |
| Chosen one with a prophecy  | The prophecy is wrong, and they know it, but everyone believes | Imposter syndrome as a character arc              |
| Brooding loner              | They're not brooding by choice — they have a contagious curse  | The loneliness is a sacrifice, not a personality  |
| Farm boy who becomes a hero | They miss the farm every single day and resent the adventure   | Hero who fights bravely but just wants to go home |

**Key insight**: You don't need to be the most original person at the table. You need to be the most **specific**. A "vengeful rogue" is a cliché. A "vengeful rogue who leaves flowers at every grave she creates" is a character.

> **Prompt to use**: "It's okay to start with a familiar concept. What cliché or archetype appeals to you? Let's take it somewhere surprising."

## The Personality-Power Nexus

**A character's abilities should feel like an extension of who they are**, not just tactical choices. When personality and powers align, the character feels _inevitable_.

| Personality Trait           | Mechanical Expression                             | Why It Works                                                      |
| --------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Chaotic, impulsive          | Explosions, wild magic, grenades                  | The fighting style mirrors the inner chaos                        |
| Calm, centered, disciplined | Shield + sword, defensive magic, precise strikes  | Control on the outside reflects control within                    |
| Nurturing, self-sacrificing | Healing, buffs, taking hits for allies            | Powers serve the emotional core                                   |
| Paranoid, distrustful       | Traps, divination, illusions                      | They fight by controlling information, because they trust nothing |
| Creative, unconventional    | Improvised weapons, multiclassing, unusual combos | They approach combat the way they approach life                   |

**The reverse also works**: Choose a class/build first, then ask "What kind of person fights this way?"

- A wizard who chose evocation (destruction magic) → Why? Are they angry? Afraid? Do they want to end fights fast because they hate violence?
- A fighter who only uses a shield (no weapon) → What happened that made them decide to never attack?

> **Prompt to use**: "How does this character's personality show up in the way they fight, cast spells, or solve problems?"

## The Simplicity Principle

**Complex backstories are not better backstories.** A simple story told with specificity and emotional truth will always outperform a convoluted epic.

**The test**: If you can't summarize the character's backstory in 2-3 sentences, it's too complex.

| Too Complex                                                                                                                                                                                                                | Just Right                                                                                                                                                                       |
| -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| "Born during a prophecy, orphaned by a war between two kingdoms, raised by monks, discovered they're the heir to one kingdom, betrayed by their mentor, fled across the sea, learned forbidden magic from a dying sage..." | "A blacksmith's daughter who left home after a fire took everything. She blames herself. Now she fixes things — walls, swords, people — because she couldn't fix what mattered." |

**Why simplicity wins:**

- The DM can integrate a simple backstory into the campaign more easily
- Other players can remember and engage with a clear concept
- There's room for the character to grow during play, instead of having everything pre-written
- Emotional resonance comes from specificity, not length

**The "Leave Room" Rule**: Your backstory is the first chapter, not the whole book. Leave mysteries unanswered, villains unfound, and questions open — that's what the campaign is for.

> **Prompt to use**: "Can you tell me your character's story in three sentences? If not, let's find the emotional core and build out from there."

## The Trait-Flaw Grid

**Every strength casts a shadow.** Instead of picking random flaws, **derive them directly from the character's greatest strengths.** This ensures the character stays consistent even when they fail.

| Strength          | Corresponding Flaw                       |
| ----------------- | ---------------------------------------- |
| **Brave**         | Reckless / Adrenaline Junkie             |
| **Loyal**         | Blind to betrayal / Codependent          |
| **Intelligent**   | Arrogant / Over-analyzes simple problems |
| **Charismatic**   | Manipulative / Craves validation         |
| **Vigilant**      | Paranoid / Can't relax                   |
| **Compassionate** | Naive / Sacrifices self too easily       |

**The method**:

1. List the character's top 3 strengths
2. Ask: "What happens when this trait goes too far?"
3. That excess becomes the flaw

> **Prompt to use**: "Your character is incredibly [STRENGTH]. In what situation does that trait actually cause a problem for them?"

## Situational Testing

**Don't just ask who the character is — TEST them.** Put the character in a hypothetical pressure cooker to reveal their true nature.

**scenarios to pose:**

- **The Mercy Test**: "An enemy surrenders, but they killed your friend. Do you spare them?"
- **The Gold Test**: "You find a pouch of 1000gp. The owner is a wealthy jerk who won't miss it. Do you return it?"
- **The Secret Test**: "You learn a truth that would destroy the party's morale but save their lives. Do you tell them?"
- **The Power Test**: "A demon offers you the power to save your dying sister, but it costs an innocent life. Do you take it?"

**Key insight**: Characters are defined by what they do when it's hard, not when it's easy.

> **Prompt to use**: "Let's test this personality. Scenario: You see a starving child stealing bread. The guards catch them. You can intervene, but you'll blow your cover. What do you do?"

## The Character Journal Entry

**Find the character's voice by writing as them.** This bypasses the analytical brain and taps into the emotional one.

**The exercise**:
Ask the player to write (or dictate) a short diary entry from the character's perspective about a specific moment:

- The night before they left home
- The morning after their biggest failure
- Trying to explain magic to a child

**Why it works**: It forces the player to choose words, rhythm, and tone. Is the entry poetic? Angry? terse? Full of spelling errors? That's the voice.

> **Prompt to use**: "I want you to write 3-4 sentences as if you are your character writing in a journal the night before the adventure begins. What are they thinking? What are they afraid to write down?"

## The Relationship Web Diagram

**Characters don't exist in a vacuum.** Visualize the network of connections.

**The method**:

1. Place the character in the center
2. Draw lines to 3-4 other entities (Party members, NPCs, Factions)
3. Label the **nature** of the line (e.g., "Respects but distrusts", "Owes a life debt", "Secretly loves")

## Few-Shot Probing Examples

When players give generic or shallow answers, use these probing patterns to dig deeper:

**Input:** "My parents died when I was young."
**Output:** "That's a tragic start. Let's dig deeper: Was it a sudden accident you witnessed, a slow illness that drained your family's savings, or did someone order it? What is the last _sound_ you remember from that day?"

**Input:** "I want to be rich and famous."
**Output:** "Gold and glory are great, but _why_? Are you trying to buy back your family's lost estate? Are you trying to prove wrong the nobles who mocked your torn clothes? What is the _real_ reason you need that gold?"

**Input:** "He's a loner who doesn't trust anyone."
**Output:** "Interesting — but _how_ did he become a loner? Was there a specific betrayal, or did trust erode slowly? And here's the key: does he _want_ to trust people but can't, or has he genuinely given up? That distinction changes everything about how he'll interact with the party."

**Key Pattern**: Always probe with **sensory memory** ("What did you hear/see/feel?"), **consequence** ("What happened because of it?"), and **blame** ("Who do they hold responsible?").

## Pillar 1: Origin

**Core Question**: Where were they born, and how did that place shape them?

Origin is not just geography — it's **atmosphere**. A character born in a bustling market reeking of fish and spices develops different instincts than one raised in a silent, snow-covered village or a golden palace with cold marble floors.

**Inspiration Framework**:

- Grenouille (_Perfume_) — born amidst chaos, developed supernatural senses
- Mowgli (_The Jungle Book_) — raised in wilderness, bonded with nature
- Batman — born in dark wealthy city, used resources to transcend origin

**Questions to Explore**:

1. What did the character see every day growing up?
2. What sounds defined their childhood?
3. Was their birthplace a source of pride or shame?
4. If they returned today, would it feel like home?

## Pillar 2: Family & Background

**Core Question**: Who raised them, and what values were absorbed before they could question them?

Even absent parents leave a mark — the mark of absence. A character raised by a loving grandmother has fundamentally different relationship patterns than one raised in an orphanage or by wolves.

**Questions to Explore**:

1. What phrase did their parent/guardian repeat constantly?
2. What family tradition do they still follow (or actively reject)?
3. Is there a family secret they don't know yet?
4. Who in their family would they die for? Who would they never forgive?

## Pillar 3: Motivations

**Core Question**: Why does this character get out of bed in the morning?

Motivation is the engine that drives every action. Without it, a character is just a puppet. The key distinction: it must be the **character's** motivation, not the **player's**.

**Motivation Types**:

- **Survival**: Running from something (debt, enemies, prophecy)
- **Seeking**: Looking for something (a person, an artifact, meaning)
- **Redemption**: Making up for a past mistake
- **Ambition**: Rising to power, fame, or mastery
- **Duty**: Fulfilling an obligation (oath, family, religion)
- **Curiosity**: The compulsion to know, explore, understand

## Pillar 4: Personality

**Core Question**: How do they perceive and react to the world?

Personality is the filter through which everything passes. Two characters can witness the same event and have completely opposite reactions based on their personality.

**Key Dimensions**:

- **Optimist vs Pessimist**: Does the glass seem half-full or half-empty?
- **Introvert vs Extrovert**: Do they recharge alone or with others?
- **Thinker vs Feeler**: Do they decide with logic or emotion?
- **Orderly vs Chaotic**: Do they plan or improvise?

---

## Pillar 5: Ideals

**Core Question**: What would they die for? What would they never do?

Ideals are the non-negotiable lines in the sand. They define not just what a character believes, but what they **refuse** to accept.

**Questions to Explore**:

1. What is their stance on authority and power?
2. How do they view violence — necessary evil, last resort, or first option?
3. What does loyalty mean to them?
4. What habit or belief contradicts their class or race stereotype?

---

## Pillar 6: Weaknesses

**Core Question**: What makes them human (or elf, or half-orc)?

Strengths create admiration. **Weaknesses create love.** An audience bonds with a character through their vulnerabilities, not their victories.

**Types of Weakness**:

- **Physical**: Limp, chronic pain, illness, disfigurement
- **Emotional**: Grief, anger, jealousy, abandonment issues
- **Social**: Awkwardness, arrogance, inability to trust
- **Intellectual**: Naivety, obsession, closed-mindedness
- **Moral**: Cowardice, selfishness, vindictiveness

---

## Pillar 7: Key Decisions

**Core Question**: What choices defined who they became?

A character's history is a collection of crossroads. Each choice creates a fork — one path taken, another abandoned. The consequences of those choices echo through the present.

**Framework**: For each key decision, document:

1. **The Situation**: What forced the choice?
2. **The Options**: What could they have done?
3. **The Choice**: What did they actually do?
4. **The Consequence**: What happened because of it?
5. **The Regret/Pride**: How do they feel about it now?

---

## Pillar 8: Friends

**Core Question**: Who chose to be in their life?

Friends are voluntary family. They reveal what a character values in others and offer the DM ready-made plot hooks.

**Friend Archetypes**:

- **The Mirror**: Reflects their best qualities back at them
- **The Opposite**: Challenges them by being everything they're not
- **The Anchor**: Keeps them grounded when things get dark
- **The Ghost**: A friend they lost who still influences them

---

## Pillar 9: Mentors

**Core Question**: Who taught them what they know, and what lesson did they reject?

Every mentor leaves two legacies: the lesson accepted and the lesson rejected. Both shape the character equally.

**Mentor Dynamics**:

- **The Living Master**: Still active, still teaching, perhaps disappointed
- **The Dead Legend**: Memory and teachings remain, but can't be questioned
- **The Fallen Mentor**: Turned to darkness, forcing a crisis of faith
- **The Reluctant Teacher**: Didn't want to teach, taught by example

---

## Pillar 10: Rivals & Enemies

**Core Question**: Who stands in their way, and why is it personal?

A great rival is not just an obstacle — they're a **dark mirror**. The best rivalries arise when both sides have valid motivations.

**Rivalry Types**:

- **Ideological**: They want the same thing through opposite means
- **Personal**: Betrayal, jealousy, or unresolved history
- **Professional**: Competing for the same goal or position
- **Philosophical**: Fundamentally different worldviews
- **Familial**: Blood ties make the conflict inescapable

## Writing Notes: Childhood Bilingualism

## Writing Notes: Childhood Bilingualism

Source: unknown

## Content

Simultaneous bilingualism: Acquisition of two languages from infancy.

## Acquisition Strategies

- The Easier Word: Children may use the easier/shorter word from one language in both (e.g. _nana_ for
 banana).
- Sound Combinations: Avoid complex sounds.
- Frequency of Use: Frequent words learned first.
- Grammatical Structures: Simple structures learned first (e.g. Italian verb endings learned earlier than
 German due to consistency).
- Consistency: Parent's consistency in language choice helps child separate systems.

## Code-Switching

- Situational: Speaking one language at home, another at school.
- Conversational: Switching within a sentence (_"Ella es bonita. She is pretty."_).
- Borrowing/Loanwords: Using words from one language in another (_"Vamos al la United Nations"_).

Note: Bilingual children acquire milestones within normal ranges. Code-switching is a sign of mastery, not
confusion.

## Theories

1. Unitary System Hypothesis: Child initially constructs one lexicon/grammar.
2. Separate Systems Hypothesis: Child builds distinct lexicon/grammar for each. (Evidence: distinct rules where languages diverge).

## Writing Notes: Culture Shock

## Writing Notes: Culture Shock

Source: unknown

## Content

Culture shock occurs when individuals move to a cultural environment different from their own.

## The 4 Phases

### 1. Honeymoon

- Differences seen in a romantic light.
- Fascination with new food, habits.
- Lasts a few weeks.

### 2. Negotiation

- Differences become apparent and create anxiety/distress.
- Excitement turns to irritation or frustration.
- Triggers: Language barriers, hygiene differences, traffic, food.
- Physical effects: Insomnia, digestion issues.
- Social effects: Loneliness, homesick.

### 3. Adjustment

- (6-12 months).
- Routines develop; life becomes "normal".
- Problem-solving skills improve.
- Negative reactions decrease.

### 4. Adaptation

- Full participation in host culture.
- Does not mean total assimilation; often results in biculturalism (keeping traits of native culture).

## Writing Notes: Fear

## Writing Notes: Fear

Source: unknown

## Content

## Synonyms

- High: Terrified, panicked, petrified.
- Moderate: Worried, tense, dread, anxious.
- Low: Uneasy, apprehensive, edgy.

## Signs

- Eyebrows pulled up and together.
- Upper eyelids up, lower eyelids tense.
- Mouth stretched back.

## Notes

- Fear vs. Anxiety: Fear is immediate/specific; Anxiety is future/diffuse.
- Fear vs. Anger (Body Language): Anger is offensive (taking space); Fear is defensive (shrinking away).

## Writing Notes: Happiness

## Writing Notes: Happiness

Source: unknown

## Content

## Synonyms

- High: Ecstatic, elated, euphoric, jubilant.
- Moderate: Cheerful, upbeat, optimistic, delighted.
- Low: Merry, glad, pleased, content.

## Signs

- Eyes squint slightly (crow's feet).
- Cheeks raise.
- Smile lines appear (sides of nose to mouth).
- Mouth corners move up diagonally.
- Mouth parts, exposing teeth.

## Notes on Smiling

- A true smile (Duchenne smile) involves the eyes.
- If the eyes don't match the mouth, the smile may be fake, cold, or hiding another emotion.
- Smiles can be warm, authentic, humorless, mocking, or cruel.

## Quotes

## Writing Notes: Grammar Development Stages

## Writing Notes: Grammar Development Stages

Source: unknown

## Content

Children master grammar rapidly between ages 1 and 4.

## Stages

1. Holophrastic Stage (12-18 months):
- One-word utterances carrying sentence meaning (e.g., "Dada", "Gone").
- 60% naming, 20% action.

2. Two-Word Stage (18 months - 2 years):
- Primitive sentences ("Cat jump", "Shut door").
- Basic word order established.

3. Filling Out (3rd year):
- Adding elements ("Daddy got car").
- Simple questions ("Where daddy put car?").

4. Sentence Stringing (Age 3):
- Longer sentences using "and", "because", "so".
- Telling simple stories.

5. Sorting Out (Age 4):
- Correcting irregularities.
- Moving from "Him gived" to "He gave".
- Learning adult forms of pronouns and irregular verbs.

6. Complex Patterns (4.5 years+):
- Subordination and complex sentence connection.
- Continues into early teens (especially written style).

## Writing Notes: Habits & Identity

## Writing Notes: Habits & Identity

Source: unknown

## Content

James Clear (_Atomic Habits_) notes "Identity" comes from _essentitas_ (being) + _identitum_ (repeatedly).
Identity = Repeatedly Being.

## Character Building

Use repeated actions (habits) to _show_ personality rather than describing it.

- Example: Instead of saying "Sam is social," show Sam habitually hosting dinners.
- Example: Instead of saying "He is selfish," show him texting while driving.

## Template Additions

Consider tracking:

- Habits: Important repeated behaviors.
- Quirks: Peculiarities.
- Behavior Patterns: Reactions to specific situations.

## Writing Notes: Facial Expressions

## Writing Notes: Facial Expressions

Source: unknown

## Content

## 7 Universal Expressions

Anger, Contempt, Fear, Disgust, Happiness/Joy, Sadness, Surprise.

## Types of Expressions

1. Macroexpressions:
- Normal daily interaction.
- Last 0.5 - 4 seconds.

2. Microexpressions:
- Conscious or unconscious concealment.
- Last less than 0.5 seconds.
- Can be spotted with training.

3. Subtle Expressions:
- Low intensity emotion or onset of emotion.
- Strongly correlated with detecting lies.

## Writing Notes: Swearing & Taboo Expressions

## Writing Notes: Swearing & Taboo Expressions

Source: unknown

## Content

Swearing intensifies speech, expresses strong feelings, or signals close relationships (or aggression).

## Categories (English)

1. Religious Taboo:
- Generally weaker.
- Examples: _Damn, Hell, Christ._

2. Body/Bodily Function Taboo:
- Generally stronger.
- Examples: _Shit, Fuck._

## Usage

- Interjections: Expressing sudden angry/strong feeling. ("Shit! I forgot.")
- Wh- Exclamatives: "Why the hell..."
- Intensifiers: Adverbs/Adjectives. ("Bloody key", "Fucking dangerous").

## Happiness

## Writing Notes: Health Definitions

## Writing Notes: Health Definitions

Source: unknown

## Content

WHO Definition: "A state of complete physical, mental and social well-being and not merely the absence of
disease or infirmity."

## Determinants

- Social/Economic environment.
- Physical environment.
- Individual behavior.

## Global Indicators (WHO)

1. Life Expectancy:
- Average time an organism is expected to live.
- Varies based on public health, diet, medical care.

2. Infant Mortality Rate (IMR):
- Deaths of children under 1 year per 1000 live births.
- Key indicator of societal health (sanitation, education, infrastructure).

3. Subjective Well-being (SWB):
- "Happiness" / Life satisfaction.
- Influenced by personality (internal) and society/needs (external).
- Can measure cultural/social health beyond just survival.

## Writing Notes: Parenting Styles (Baumrind)

## Writing Notes: Parenting Styles (Baumrind)

Source: unknown

## Content

Defined by Responsiveness (warmth) and Demandingness (control).

## 1. Authoritarian (Too Hard)

- High Demand, Low Response.
- Rigid, harsh, "Because I said so."
- Outcomes: Anxious, withdrawn.
- Cultural Note: In some cultures (e.g., Chinese American), this style does not correlate with negative
 outcomes as it does in Western samples.

## 2. Permissive (Too Soft)

- Low Demand, High Response.
- No rules, "Spoiled child."
- Outcomes: Lack of discipline, poor grades, risky behavior.
- Positives: High self-esteem, better social skills.

## 3. Authoritative (Just Right)

- High Demand, High Response.
- Firm but flexible, supportive.
- Outcomes: High self-esteem, social skills (in Western context).

## Writing Notes: The Five-Factor Model of Personality

## Writing Notes: The Five-Factor Model of Personality

Source: unknown

## Content

Culture is transmitted through language and social norms. Psychologists use the Five-Factor Model (FFM) or
OCEAN to understand personality, though its applicability across all cultures is debated (Western bias).

## The 5 Personality Traits (OCEAN)

### OPENNESS TO EXPERIENCE

Refers to a person's imagination, feelings, actions, ideas.

- LOW: Practical, conventional, prefer routine.
- HIGH: Curious, wide range of interests, independent.

### CONSCIENTIOUSNESS

Competence, self-discipline, thoughtfulness, goal-driven.

- LOW: Impulsive, careless, disorganized.
- HIGH: Hardworking, dependable, organized.

### EXTROVERSION

Sociability, assertiveness, emotional expression.

- LOW: Quiet, reserved, withdrawn.
- HIGH: Outgoing, warm, seeks adventure.

### AGREEABLENESS

Cooperative, trustworthy, good-natured.

- LOW: Critical, uncooperative, suspicious.
- HIGH: Helpful, trusting, empathetic.

### NEUROTICISM

Tendency toward unstable emotions.

- LOW: Calm, even-tempered, secure.
- HIGH: Anxious, unhappy, prone to negative emotions.

## Cultural Research

- Etic traits: Universal biological bases. Big Five should appear across all cultures.
- Emic traits: Unique to specific cultures.
- Research using the NEO-PI test has found support for the FFM in many cultures (Chinese, Dutch, Italian,
 etc.), especially when using third-party reporting to avoid self-report bias.

## Writing Notes: Beyond East & West Differences of Personality

## Writing Notes: Beyond East & West Differences of Personality

Source: unknown

## Content

Research challenges the simple dichotomy of West (Independent) vs East (Interdependent).

## Vignoles' & Colleagues Study (2016)

- Studied 55 cultural groups across 33 countries.
- Identified 7 dimensions of self-construal:
1. Difference
2. Connection
3. Self-Direction
4. Self-Reliance
5. Consistency
6. Self-Expression
7. Self-Interest

## Key Findings

- Western cultures are NOT wholly independent. They scored high on difference/self-expression but low on
 self-reliance and consistency.
- Latin American cultures are NOT wholly interdependent. They scored high on consistency and self-interest.
- Poorest samples scored highest on self-interest (challenging the view that rich Westerners are selfish).
- Religious heritage matters: Muslim and Catholic samples scored high on consistency.

## Conclusion

- Self-construal is NOT binary.
- It is a complex interaction of heritage, socioeconomic development, settlement patterns, and ecological
 contexts.

## Character Development: Unlikable to Likable

## Character Development: Unlikable to Likable

Source: unknown

## Content

3 strategies to make unlikable characters likable (or at least more relatable).

## 1. Redeeming Quality

- Give your hero one redeeming quality or action (even if small) at the beginning.
- Helps endear readers to them.

## 2. An Enemy

- Give your hero a really evil enemy.
- Readers sympathize with an unlikable character when compared to someone even _more_ unlikable.

## 3. Love to Hate

- Hardest to implement.
- Find a unique flaw or skill to exploit/exaggerate to the point of comedy, ridicule, or fascination.

## Writing Notes: Culture

## Writing Notes: Culture

Source: unknown

## Content

Culture: Patterns of learned and shared behavior that are cumulative and transmitted across generations.

## Key Aspects

1. Patterns: Systematic, predictable ways of behavior/thinking. (e.g. Gift giving customs).
2. Sharing: Product of people sharing knowledge/skills.
3. Learned: Acquired via enculturation (caregivers, rituals). Not innate.
- _Rituals:_ Teach importance (e.g. Bar/Bat Mitzvah).
4. Cumulative: Stored knowledge grows across generations.
5. Transmission: Passing knowledge to next generation (primarily in the home).

## Implications

- Conflict: Different learned behaviors cause tension (e.g. technology use).
- Self-Awareness: Understanding our preferences are learned empowering us to revise them.
- Adaptation: Culture changes to meet needs (migration, resource loss).

## Writing Notes: Two Views of Culture

## Writing Notes: Two Views of Culture

Source: unknown

## Content

## 1. ETIC (Universal)

- Cultural Universals: Processes existing in _every_ culture.
- Examples: Language, Group membership, Ritual, Emotions.
- Focus of general psychology.

## 2. EMIC (Culturally Specific)

- Focuses on cultural differences and specific processes.
- "Insider's" perspective.
- Risk: Bias if researcher doesn't consider outside perceptions.

## Products of Culture

- Material Culture: Objects (food, fashion, architecture).
- Nonmaterial Culture: Ideas, attitudes, beliefs.
- Norms: Rules for behavior in specific situations (e.g. funeral attire).
- Values: Global/abstract judgments of good/evil (e.g. patriotism).
- Beliefs: How the universe operates (religious or secular).

## Writing Notes: Culture Shock

## Writing Notes: Culture Shock

Source: unknown

## Content

Culture shock occurs when individuals move to a cultural environment different from their own.

## The 4 Phases

### 1. Honeymoon

- Differences seen in a romantic light.
- Fascination with new food, habits.
- Lasts a few weeks.

### 2. Negotiation

- Differences become apparent and create anxiety/distress.
- Excitement turns to irritation or frustration.
- Triggers: Language barriers, hygiene differences, traffic, food.
- Physical effects: Insomnia, digestion issues.
- Social effects: Loneliness, homesick.

### 3. Adjustment

- (6-12 months).
- Routines develop; life becomes "normal".
- Problem-solving skills improve.
- Negative reactions decrease.

### 4. Adaptation

- Full participation in host culture.
- Does not mean total assimilation; often results in biculturalism (keeping traits of native culture).

## Writing Notes: Frameworks of Health

## Writing Notes: Frameworks of Health

Source: unknown

## Content

## 1. Biomedical Model (Western)

- Focus: Physical processes (pathology, biochemistry).
- Health = Freedom from disease/pain/defect.
- Ignores social/subjective factors.

## 2. Traditional Medicine (Holistic)

- Focus: Knowledge/skills based on indigenous theories and beliefs.
- Often more holistic.

## 3. Biopsychosocial Model (Engel, 1977)

- Focus: Interconnection of 3 factors.
- Biological: Genetic, biochemical.
- Psychological: Mood, personality, behavior.
- Social: Culture, family, socioeconomic status.
- Example: Depression caused by genetics (Bio) + perfectionism (Psycho) + work stress (Social).

## Writing Notes: Mutism

## Writing Notes: Mutism

Source: unknown

## Content

Definition: Inability or unwillingness to speak.

## Types

1. Selective Mutism: Ability to speak exists, but anxiety prevents it in specific social situations (e.g., school). Associated with social anxiety.
2. Organic Mutism: Caused by brain injury/stroke/drugs.
3. Cerebellar Mutism: Caused by surgery/tumor in cerebellum.
4. Aphasia: Difficulty speaking due to brain damage (often accompanied by reading/writing issues).

## Characteristics for Writing

- Selective: Speaks at home, silent at school/with strangers. Paralyzing anxiety. Nonverbal communication
 used.
- Organic/Aphasia: Frustration (wanting to speak but unable).
- General symptoms: Social withdrawal, impact on school/work, nonverbal gestures.

## Note

Selective mutism differs from shyness; it is a consistent failure to speak in specific settings for at least 1
month.

## Writing Notes: Parenting Styles (Baumrind)

## Writing Notes: Parenting Styles (Baumrind)

Source: unknown

## Content

Defined by Responsiveness (warmth) and Demandingness (control).

## 1. Authoritarian (Too Hard)

- High Demand, Low Response.
- Rigid, harsh, "Because I said so."
- Outcomes: Anxious, withdrawn.
- Cultural Note: In some cultures (e.g., Chinese American), this style does not correlate with negative
 outcomes as it does in Western samples.

## 2. Permissive (Too Soft)

- Low Demand, High Response.
- No rules, "Spoiled child."
- Outcomes: Lack of discipline, poor grades, risky behavior.
- Positives: High self-esteem, better social skills.

## 3. Authoritative (Just Right)

- High Demand, High Response.
- Firm but flexible, supportive.
- Outcomes: High self-esteem, social skills (in Western context).

## Writing Notes: Generations (Ortega y Gasset)

## Writing Notes: Generations (Ortega y Gasset)

Source: unknown

## Content

Generations are shaped by biology, history, and social institutions.

## 5 Generations

1. Childhood (0-15): Learning.
2. Youth (15-30): Preparation/Action.
3. Initiation (30-45): Constructive work.
4. Dominance (45-60): Power/Control.
5. Old Age (60+): Legacy/Decline.

## Key Concept

Relationships between generations are crucial in all societies. We carry aspects of every generation within
us.

## Writing Notes: Thinking & Categorization

## Writing Notes: Thinking & Categorization

Source: unknown

## Content

Concept: Mental representations (e.g., "Chair") that allow us to predict and interact with the world.

## Hierarchy of Categories

1. Superordinate: General (e.g., Animal). Hardest for children to learn.
2. Basic: Generic, most salient differences (e.g., Dog). First learned, fastest identification.
3. Subordinate: Specific (e.g., Labrador).

## Cultural Differences

_Based on research by Chiu (1972), Norenzayan et al. (2002), etc._

### Holistic Thinking (East Asian / Collectivist)

- Focus: Relationships, context, environment.
- Categorization: By relationship (e.g., Cow + Grass because cows eat grass).
- Perception: Sees the "whole picture."

### Analytic Thinking (Western / Individualist)

- Focus: Individual objects, specific attributes.
- Categorization: By similarity/rule (e.g., Cow + Chicken because both are animals).
- Perception: Separates object from context.
